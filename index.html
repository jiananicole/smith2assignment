NYC Landmark Designations by Borough

Staten Island, Bronx received least landmark designations in past decade

The Old Croton Aqueduct Walk in the Bronx. Photo courtesy of Eggar Almonte, TrailLink.
by Jiana Smith
December 19, 2023

The Bronx’s Old Croton Aqueduct Walk, a park built over a water source which helped 19th-century New Yorkers access clean water during an outbreak of cholera, was calendared for potential historical landmark designation by the NYC Landmark Preservation Committee (LPC) in August. If designated, the Aqueduct Walk will be the borough’s first ever scenic landmark.

Over the past decade, the LPC, which recognizes and preserves historical sites from harm, has designated around 172 sites around the city. However, of these sites, the Bronx and Staten Island have received the least designations, at 10 and eight sites respectively, according to data from the organization.

Historically savvy citizens of Staten Island expressed concern about this low number back in 2020, after the LPC decided not to consider the Bayley Seton Hospital for designation.

Overall, Manhattan has seen the most activity, with around 90 designations since 2013. Queens, which overall has the least landmarks, is now rapidly catching up, with 16 designations in the past decade.

Another Bronx site, the Joseph Rodman Drake and Enslaved People’s Burial Ground in Hunts Point, is one of the city’s newest landmarks. But this designation only came after a decade-long fight for its recognition by the Hunts Point Slave Burial Ground Project.

The Bronx and Staten Island’s low designation count begs the question: What areas are worthy of historic recognition and why?
